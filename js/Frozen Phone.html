Frozen Phone


Jack S. is a mundane “corporate slave”.

“Mediocre in every aspect. According to his colleagues, he is fine in everything, but just a little introvert."

— The office phone rang.

Jack sits in front of the computer, raises his head, and observes the office silently. He usually enjoys the vacant office the most: no one talking, distance between each other, and no one ever passes by his desk during the whole evening. He loathes the most every rushed afternoon, that men and women passing by causes the back of his chair tremble, bringing up a whirlwind- like air current, as if he was touched by white walkers, freezed him as stiff as a corpse.

Recalling that goosebump experience, Jack slightly curled up in fear.

The distance from the office ring to Jack’s seat is in a subtle situation——neither far nor close. He can shrink under the three “defensive walls”, made by the plastic glass, struggling with his survival instinct in this small box.

The telephone bell keeps in a precise volume, it sounds like a tsunami in the empty office, wave after wave; in the eardrum of Jack, it is like an ominous bell that woke calamity, every echo sounds like thunder. “Blood” drops on Jack’s forehead, almost as if he is the pessimistic protagonist in Shakespeare tragedy, choosing「To be, or not to be?」 The telephone ring suddenly stops.

It was a colleague sitting in another corner answering it due to the continuing noise. As she is picking the phone up, she seems to take a glance at Jack: his pale, sweaty cheeks flushes under the gaze.

Jack soothes his deafening heartbeat, and in the short fifty seconds, he feels as if he has experienced life and death all over again.

Why Telephone?

It is said that when mobile phones were first introduced into common use, people thought making a phone call was a very private behavior. At that time, those with posh living conditions could choose to nest on the sofa at home, in front of the receiver in an empty bedroom or living room, and leisurely chat about life or work.

For a long time, few people chose to talk on the phone in public because it meant that conversations between two people were no longer private nor only between themselves, which would be fatal to any form of relationships. A call in public was like a sign to invite others to listen, to spy on your conversations. The attention of the passers-by was like Damocles's sword, quivering on your spine, as if it was an enduring painful torture.

And sometimes the phone call is like a lottery box. The caller’s identity could be unknown to the receiver. The topic of the conversation can also be unpredictable. And if there is a misused word or awkward silence, people’s condemnation, a mere sneer, or even one imagination of that would shatter one’s bottom line. For some patients of social anxiety, the tension of the unknown is like waiting in a hospital for a definitive verdict. This small, potentially awful scenario makes people fall in the abyss of dilemma, between answering the phone and not answering it.

On the End: Where we are not living in a binary world, there’s no normal or not normal.

What is SAD?

"Social anxiety", also known as social phobia, has become a commonly used slang. "Social anxiety" or embarrassing scenes are everywhere. Public speaking, called on by teachers, listening to your boss singing in karaoke, or entangling with the sight of strangers may be all defined as mild triggers.

It’s just for SAD that their normal lives have been severely affected. For most people, speaking on a conference may give them a sweaty palm at most, but for SAD patients, this may be a catalyst for their suicide.

With reference to the psychological theory that “Neurological tendencies aren't binary ticker boxes, they're just scaled differently on a spectrum." , it is a human right to feel embarrassed. In any case, everyone has a manifestation of individual differences, just some people have a bit more.

Respect differences. Accept differences.
